# Crafting a Winning Hackathon Entry: A Step-by-Step Guide to Building a Teacher-Centric AI Tool with GPT-5.6

## The Judging Imperative: An Equity-First Vision for AI in Education

The success of an OpenAI DevPost Education-track submission hinges on demonstrating a credible, human-centered vision for learning—not simply deploying impressive technology. Leah Belsky's public work and education leadership at OpenAI point toward an equity-first standard: AI should expand access to high-quality learning and help people who have less access to teacher support or learning resources [[24,90,201,255]]. This vision is broader than automation. A strong submission should show a tangible pathway to closing an educational gap, with technical choices serving that outcome. Efficiency matters, but it is most persuasive when it gives teachers more capacity to support learners who would otherwise be left behind.

For this project, that implication is clear: make multilingual access the central outcome and teacher time savings the enabling mechanism. Rather than presenting a broad collection of AI features, the product should make one promise that a judge can see in a single demo: *a teacher can turn an existing lesson into an understandable, culturally considerate learning package for a student who does not yet speak the classroom language fluently.* That is a specific, globally relevant expression of inclusive AI—and a more compelling story than a generic lesson-content generator.

A central tenet of Belsky's philosophy is the primacy of pedagogy over pure technology [[59]]. She has consistently emphasized that the crucial question is not merely about providing tool access, but about changing learning, cognition, and teaching practices [[28]]. This perspective directly informs her warnings against students using AI as a simple "answer machine" [[23,180]]. She draws a parallel to misusing a calculator, suggesting that the true value lies in using AI as a scaffold for critical thinking and creativity, not as a substitute for it [[43,45]]. Therefore, a project that passively generates answers is less compelling than one that fosters deeper cognitive processes, such as metacognition or evaluative reasoning [[238,248]]. The ideal submission will showcase how its tool guides users toward a better understanding of a subject or a more effective learning process, thereby preserving the "productive friction" that is essential for genuine learning [[270,292]]. The narrative should highlight how the product supports, rather than replaces, the educator's judgment and the student's intellectual agency.

Accessibility and inclusion are paramount in Belsky's framework for AI in education. She has been a vocal advocate for addressing the significant language barriers that hinder learning for millions globally. Citing data that 40% of the world's population lacks access to education in a language they understand, she has highlighted this issue as a fundamental challenge [[30,31,280]]. Her public discussions about improving AI interactions for Indian students and teachers in local languages underscore a commitment to culturally and linguistically responsive AI solutions [[89,175]]. Furthermore, she champions AI's role in supporting diverse learners, including students with special educational needs and English Language Learners (ELLs) [[57,222,254]]. Any project that demonstrably breaks down barriers for multilingual or neurodiverse students by providing personalized scaffolding, translation, or alternative content formats will strongly align with her values and the spirit of the Education track. This focus on equitable access is also reflected in OpenAI's partnerships, such as the collaboration with the American Federation of Teachers to train educators and ensure responsible adoption [[12,167]].

Finally, empowering educators is a cornerstone of Belsky's strategy. While much of the conversation around AI in schools focuses on students, Belsky recognizes that teachers are the linchpins of successful implementation. She has pointed out that AI tools can help teachers not only create materials tailored to individual student needs but also track their progress effectively [[50]]. Projects that alleviate the immense administrative burden on teachers, streamline lesson planning, and enhance instructional efficiency are highly relevant [[141,182]]. The fact that 53% of K-12 teachers report burnout, often linked to tasks like lesson planning and grading, presents a clear and urgent need that AI can address [[35]]. A project that positions itself as a tool to augment a teacher's expertise, protect their time, and enable them to focus more on student engagement and personalized instruction will be viewed favorably [[143,321]]. The overall goal, as articulated by Belsky, is to make AI a powerful assistant to great teaching, not a replacement for it [[254]]. Consequently, a submission's ability to articulate a clear value proposition for teachers—making their work more manageable, efficient, and impactful—is a critical factor for success.

## Identifying High-Impact Educational Pain Points: A Data-Driven Approach

To develop a project that addresses a real-world problem, it is essential to ground the concept in the most pressing and well-documented challenges within the education system. The provided sources highlight several systemic issues, with teacher workload and student diversity emerging as the most significant. A solution targeting these areas will have a strong foundation in pedagogical relevance and practical necessity. The most acute problem is the overwhelming workload and resulting burnout among teachers. Data reveals that teachers work an average of 49 hours per week, a figure that has decreased slightly from previous years but remains exceptionally high [[218]]. A significant portion of this time is consumed by non-instructional tasks, with lesson planning being a particularly burdensome activity [[38,215]]. In some regions, full-time teachers spend upwards of eight hours per week preparing lessons alone [[282,283]]. This relentless demand is a primary driver of teacher burnout, reported by 53% of K-12 educators, with anxiety, lesson planning, and grading identified as key stressors [[35]]. The consequences extend beyond individual well-being; high teacher turnover undermines student achievement and places a heavy financial strain on school districts [[41]]. Globally, there is an urgent need for 44 million new primary and secondary teachers by 2030 [[217]], making the retention of current educators a matter of existential importance for the system. A project that offers a tangible solution to reduce this cognitive load and save valuable teacher time directly addresses a foundational crisis in education.

Complementing the issue of teacher overwhelm is the profound challenge of differentiated instruction. Modern classrooms are increasingly diverse, requiring teachers to tailor their instruction to meet the varied academic levels, cultural backgrounds, and learning needs of every student [[40]]. This task is immensely complex and time-consuming. It is a significant source of stress for educators, especially when modifying lessons for students with special educational needs (SEN) [[29]]. The scale of this challenge is growing, with the percentage of teachers working in schools where over 10% of students require SEN support rising from 30% in 2018 to 45% in 2024 [[221]]. Despite their best efforts, many teachers feel unprepared to manage this diversity; a survey found that 71% of U.S. K-12 teachers lack formal AI training, and 85% feel unprepared to manage generative AI in their classrooms [[188]]. This gap between the need for differentiation and the available resources highlights a critical opportunity for an AI-powered tool. A system capable of rapidly generating adapted content—such as materials at varying reading levels, translated into different languages, or formatted for accessibility—could empower teachers to implement differentiated instruction more effectively and efficiently, thereby improving outcomes for all learners.

Perhaps the most significant global equity issue is the pervasive language barrier to learning. For 40% of the world's population, education is delivered in a language they do not speak or understand fluently [[30,31]]. This linguistic mismatch has a devastating impact on learning outcomes; children learning in a second language are 30% less likely to achieve basic proficiency compared to those taught in their mother tongue [[106]]. This problem disproportionately affects children from Indigenous communities and linguistic minorities, creating a cycle of educational disadvantage [[144,146]]. While AI-driven translation tools, text-to-speech features, and adaptive assessments offer a promising pathway to breaking down these barriers, their adoption is uneven [[57,223,310]]. A project focused on creating an accessible, reliable, and easy-to-use tool for multilingual learning would have a profound social impact and align perfectly with Leah Belsky's advocacy for inclusive AI [[89,175]]. Such a tool could transform the classroom experience for millions of students and their families, fostering a sense of belonging and opening up access to knowledge [[147]]. Finally, while access to AI is no longer a barrier, a new challenge has emerged: the "capability overhang," where students struggle to use AI effectively even though they can easily access it [[66]]. There is a risk that students may misuse AI as a shortcut, hindering the development of critical skills [[180]]. Educators express concern that over-reliance on AI can diminish connections between students and peers and between students and teachers [[244]]. This creates a need for tools that not only provide information but also teach students how to think with AI, evaluate its outputs, and integrate it into their learning process responsibly. A project that scaffolds this higher-order skill development would be highly innovative and directly address a contemporary concern in educational practice.

## Leveraging GPT-5.6 and Codex: Mapping Capabilities to Pedagogical Needs

The selection of GPT-5.6 and Codex is a strategic choice, as their advanced capabilities provide a robust technical foundation for addressing the high-impact educational problems identified. These models offer a suite of features far beyond simple text generation, enabling the creation of sophisticated, agentic, and integrated applications. The flagship model, GPT-5.6 Sol, is designed for complex professional tasks and is supported across ChatGPT, Codex, and the OpenAI API [[77,79]]. Its key strengths include state-of-the-art performance in coding, research, science, cybersecurity, design, and multi-tool workflows [[79,80]]. For a hackathon submission, demonstrating a deep understanding of these capabilities is crucial. One of the most powerful new features is `ultra` mode, which allows the model to coordinate four sub-agents in parallel to accelerate complex tasks [[77,235]]. This agentic performance is ideal for orchestrating multi-step workflows, such as analyzing a document, generating multiple derivative versions, and formatting them for output—all within a single, cohesive process. Another key feature is `max` reasoning effort, which gives the model more time to explore alternatives and engage in deeper thought, leading to higher-quality and more nuanced outputs [[76,77]]. This would be particularly useful for tasks requiring careful pedagogical judgment, such as adapting a complex concept for a lower grade level.

Furthermore, GPT-5.6 introduces Programmatic Tool Calling, a significant advancement that enables the model to write and run lightweight programs to coordinate external tools, process intermediate results, and adapt its workflow dynamically [[77]]. This capability reduces the latency of complex tasks that would otherwise require multiple back-and-forth calls to the model. For example, a tool could instruct GPT-5.6 to first use a file search tool to read a document, then invoke a web search tool for additional context, and finally use a code interpreter to perform calculations or reformat data before generating a final response. This level of automation showcases a sophisticated use of the technology that goes beyond simple prompting. The improved coding and computer use capabilities of GPT-5.6, especially when paired with Codex, are also central to its power. Codex now supports Computer Use on both Windows and macOS, allowing it to see, click, and type within applications, as well as control browser actions [[154]]. This opens up possibilities for creating tools that can automate repetitive tasks within software environments, a feature that can be demonstrated in a hackathon setting. The large context window of the GPT-5.6 Sol model, at 1.05 million tokens, allows it to process and reason over very large documents, such as entire textbooks or lengthy lesson plans, in a single pass [[79]]. This is a critical feature for any application involving document analysis and adaptation.

The master model would then synthesize these outputs into a cohesive package for the teacher. This demonstrates a sophisticated grasp of the technology and a direct, elegant solution to a major pain point in education. The availability of these models through the API and Codex provides the necessary infrastructure for rapid prototyping and development within a hackathon timeframe [[79,206]].

## Project Concept: "Project Linguist" — Multilingual Learning Access for Every Classroom

Based on the equity and pedagogical priorities above, the strongest concept is **Project Linguist**: a teacher-reviewed tool that converts an existing classroom lesson into a multilingual learning package. It addresses teacher workload, but its north-star outcome is more important: a learner who does not yet understand the language of instruction can participate meaningfully in the same lesson as their peers. This directly targets the language barrier affecting 40% of the world's population [[30,31]], while preserving the teacher's judgment over what is taught and how the final materials are used.

The primary user is a K–12 classroom teacher with multilingual learners in a mixed-ability class [[35,218]]. The painful moment is immediate and familiar: tomorrow's worksheet is ready, but adapting it for one or more students would take too long. The teacher uploads or pastes a source lesson, chooses a target language and reading level, and receives a reviewable package: a faithful translation, a simplified-language version, a bilingual key-vocabulary list, and guided comprehension prompts. The teacher approves, edits, or rejects each item before use. This is a focused workflow that reduces the hours spent adapting materials [[38,282]] without positioning AI as an autonomous instructor [[48,136]].

The hackathon MVP should deliberately be one complete vertical slice, not three separate generators:

1. **Input:** Paste a passage or upload a short worksheet; select target language and learner reading level.
2. **Generate:** Create a source-grounded multilingual package with translation, simplified English, essential vocabulary, and three comprehension prompts.
3. **Review:** Surface the original and each adaptation side by side; let the teacher edit, approve, or regenerate individual sections.
4. **Export:** Produce a clean, printable student handout.

Advanced differentiation and interactive scaffolding remain valuable follow-on features, but they should be presented as future work unless the core multilingual workflow is polished and reliable. This constraint makes the impact legible: the demo begins with a lesson a student cannot access and ends with a teacher-approved resource that lets them join it.

This concept is technically feasible within a hackathon timeframe by leveraging the OpenAI API with the `gpt-5.6-sol` model. The backend logic would involve parsing user inputs, constructing sophisticated prompts that specify the desired transformations, and managing the interaction with the API. The frontend could be a simple web application built with modern JavaScript frameworks or even a static site with dynamic behavior. The project's strength lies in its clear, high-impact problem statement, its alignment with the judge's priorities, and its practical, achievable scope. It demonstrates a mature understanding of both the educational landscape and the power of the available AI technology.

## Implementation Roadmap: From Ideation to Submission

A successful hackathon project requires a structured development process. This roadmap outlines a phased approach, from initial validation to final polishing, designed to be executed efficiently. Each phase includes specific milestones and actionable steps to guide the development process.

**Phase 1: Ideation and Validation (Estimated Time: 1 Week)**

The primary goal of this phase is to solidify the project's core value proposition by grounding it in the actual needs of its target users. This prevents building a solution for a hypothetical problem.
*   **Week 1 Milestone:** A validated user workflow and a prioritized list of MVP features.
*   **Actionable Steps:**
    1.  **User Persona Definition:** Create a detailed profile of the target user, "Ms. Johnson," a middle school teacher specializing in history/social studies. Note her typical daily tasks, pain points, and technological comfort level.
    2.  **Empathetic Interviews (Simulated):** Conduct interviews with teachers or simulate conversations based on existing research. Ask open-ended questions to uncover unmet needs: "Walk me through the last time you had to adapt a textbook reading for your English Language Learner students." "What's the most frustrating part of trying to differentiate a single worksheet for 30 students?" "If you had a magic button that could instantly make a resource more accessible, what would you want it to do?"
    3.  **Feature Prioritization:** Synthesize interview findings to define the core features of the MVP. Based on the identified pain points, prioritize the features for Project Linguist: Language Adaptation, Content Differentiation, and Interactive Scaffolding.
    4.  **Technical Feasibility Check:** Confirm that GPT-5.6's capabilities, such as its large context window for processing documents and its agentic features for complex tasks, are sufficient for the proposed MVP. Plan to use the Codex CLI or the OpenAI API for backend operations [[79,312]].

**Phase 2: Architectural Design and Prototyping (Estimated Time: 1 Week)**

This phase focuses on designing the application's structure and creating a functional prototype to test the core logic.
*   **Week 2 Milestone:** A working prototype with a basic frontend and a backend connected to the OpenAI API.
*   **Actionable Steps:**
    1.  **UI/UX Wireframing:** Sketch the user interface for the main dashboard, including input fields for text/document upload, dropdown menus for selecting adaptation types (e.g., grade level, language), and output panels for displaying the AI-generated content.
    2.  **Frontend Development:** Build the user interface using a simple static HTML/CSS/JavaScript setup. The goal is functionality over aesthetic perfection. Ensure the UI is intuitive and clearly communicates the available options to the user.
    3.  **Backend Setup:** Initialize a server-side environment using Python with Flask or FastAPI. Install the official OpenAI API library.
    4.  **Core Logic Integration:** Write the functions that handle user input, construct the API requests to GPT-5.6, and display the responses. Start with the simplest feature, such as the Language Adaptation module, to ensure the basic communication loop works correctly. Craft a detailed prompt for this task, specifying the desired output format (e.g., JSON with keys for 'simplified_english' and 'translation').

**Phase 3: Core Feature Implementation (Estimated Time: 2 Weeks)**

This is the most intensive development phase, where all planned MVP features are built and integrated.
*   **Weeks 3-4 Milestone:** A fully functional application with all core features implemented and tested.
*   **Actionable Steps:**
    1.  **Advanced Prompt Engineering:** Develop sophisticated prompts for each feature. For the Content Differentiation Engine, the prompt must instruct GPT-5.6 to analyze the document's structure and apply changes accordingly. Use the `max` reasoning parameter to improve the quality of the generated text [[77]].
    2.  **PDF Parsing Implementation:** Integrate a library like PyPDF2 or pdfplumber to extract text from uploaded PDF files. Handle potential errors, such as scanned documents or unusual formatting.
    3.  **Agentic Workflow Demonstration (Optional but Recommended):** For a more advanced demonstration, implement a workflow using `programmatic tool calling` for the Content Differentiation Engine [[77]]. The prompt would ask GPT-5.6 to write a small script that identifies question numbers and then generates a new document with modified text, showcasing a deep understanding of the technology.
    4.  **Iterative Testing:** Continuously test each feature as it is developed. Have the team members use the tool with various sample inputs to find bugs and gather feedback on the usability of the interface and the quality of the outputs.

**Phase 4: Data Management and Risk Mitigation (Ongoing)**

Ethical considerations and data handling are critical components of the project's design and presentation.
*   **Weeks 1-5 Milestone:** A clear data policy and a set of mitigation strategies for potential risks.
*   **Actionable Steps:**
    1.  **Define Data Inputs:** Clarify that the system processes only user-provided, non-sensitive classroom materials. No personally identifiable information (PII) should be collected or stored.
    2.  **Bias and Hallucination Mitigation:** Acknowledge in the project's README and pitch that the tool uses a Large Language Model, which can produce biased or inaccurate information [[48,136]]. Frame the tool as a "drafting assistant" and include a disclaimer in the UI reminding users to review all outputs critically.
    3.  **Privacy Compliance:** Adhere to standards like FERPA and COPPA where applicable [[266]]. The tool should be designed to be used with anonymized or generic classroom content.
    4.  **Equity Statement:** Articulate how the tool promotes equity by providing every teacher with the means to support diverse learners, regardless of their own linguistic or pedagogical expertise.

## Final Polish and Strategic Presentation for Maximum Impact

The final stage of the project lifecycle is dedicated to refining the product and crafting a compelling narrative for the judges. This phase is about packaging the technical work into a persuasive story that highlights its impact, innovation, and alignment with the hackathon's goals. The submission must not only function but also communicate its value clearly and concisely. The first step is to prepare a Devpost submission that tells a coherent story. Begin by framing the problem with powerful statistics: the 49-hour work week for teachers [[218]] and the 40% of the global population denied education in their native language [[30]]. This immediately establishes the significance of the problem. Then, introduce the solution—"Project Linguist"—and explain how it directly addresses these issues. Crucially, connect the project's features to Leah Belsky's vision. Explicitly mention how the tool empowers teachers by saving them time [[182]] and how it promotes educational equity by breaking down language barriers for all learners [[90,254]]. This narrative linkage is essential for capturing the attention of the judging panel.

A high-quality video demonstration is a mandatory component of a competitive submission. The video should be short (under 2 minutes) and follow a clear workflow. Show the user interface, demonstrate uploading a sample document (e.g., a paragraph from a history textbook), select adaptation options (e.g., translate to Spanish and simplify to a 6th-grade level), and show the polished output. Narrate the video to explain each step and emphasize the time saved. For instance, state, "In our test case, our tool reduced the estimated time to create a differentiated worksheet from 45 minutes to under five." Quantifying the impact, even if it's an estimate, makes the value proposition tangible. The judges' instructions advise providing clean instructions and sample data for them to run the project themselves, so ensure the GitHub repository is well-documented with a clear `README.md` file explaining how to install dependencies and run the application locally [[273]].

Finally, prepare for the live presentation. Even if the submission is pre-recorded, being ready to pitch the project verbally is a good practice. The pitch should be concise, ideally no longer than two minutes, and cover the problem, the solution, the key features, and the impact [[298]]. Practice delivering the pitch to ensure clarity and confidence. Be prepared to answer questions from the judges about the technology stack, the ethical considerations of using LLMs, the limitations of the current model, and future directions for the project. Demonstrating a thoughtful and nuanced understanding of these topics will significantly strengthen the submission. By meticulously executing this final polish, the project transitions from a functional prototype to a polished, compelling, and strategically-positioned entry, maximizing its potential to achieve a top-30 placement.
